Chapter 1: First-Order Differential Equations

Through my studies, I've organized first-order DEs into three key categories each with distinct solution approaches and real-world applications:

1. Separable Equations

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = g(x)h(y) \]

My solution approach:

\[ \int \frac{1}{h(y)} \, dy = \int g(x) \, dx + C \]

I applied this to model antibiotic effectiveness in biological systems, where the decay rate depends on current concentration.

2. Exact Equations

\[ M(x,y)dx + N(x,y)dy = 0 \]

I discovered these are crucial in thermodynamics for calculating work done in reversible processes.

3. Linear Equations

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x) \]

I used integrating factors to solve circuit analysis problems in my electronics lab.

Numerical Methods

Euler's Method in Practice

Euler’s Method approximates solutions using:

yₙ₊₁ = yₙ + h·f(xₙ, yₙ)

While implementing this, I learned that step size choice balances accuracy vs computation cost:

Error ∝ h²
My Finding: Small step sizes (h = 0.01) give smooth approximations but require 10× more computations than h = 1.

Runge-Kutta Method (RK4)

RK4 improves accuracy by combining multiple slope estimates:

k₁ = f(xₙ, yₙ)
k₂ = f(xₙ + h/2, yₙ + (h/2)·k₁)
k₃ = f(xₙ + h/2, yₙ + (h/2)·k₂)
k₄ = f(xₙ + h, yₙ + h·k₃)

yₙ₊₁ = yₙ + (h/6)(k₁ + 2k₂ + 2k₃ + k₄)

Used in your lab code for Newton's Law of Cooling (see Lab section).

Runge-Kutta methods are the backbone of scientific computing.

Linear Differential Equations

General Linear Form

\[ a(x)\frac{dy}{dx} + b(x)y = f(x) \]

Linear DEs appear in circuits, population models, and chemical reactions.

Integrating Factor Method

\[ \mu(x) = e^{\int P(x)dx} \]

Multiply both sides by \(\mu(x)\) to make the equation integrable.

Example: Newton's Law of Cooling

Problem Statement

A cup of coffee at 90°C cools in a room at 25°C. The cooling constant is k = 0.1 min⁻¹. We want to find the temperature after 5 minutes using Euler’s method and RK4 method, with a step size of 1 minute.

Step 1: Differential Equation

Newton’s Law of Cooling:
dT/dt = -k (T - T_room), where T_room = 25°C, k = 0.1.

Step 2: Euler’s Method

Apply the update formula:
Tₙ₊₁ = Tₙ + h * (-0.1 * (Tₙ - 25))
After 5 steps, we get an approximate final temperature: ~63.4°C.

Step 3: RK4 Method

Using RK4 formulas over 5 steps, we obtain an approximate final temperature: ~63.6°C.

Step 4: Comparison

Method Temperature (°C after 5 min)
Euler 63.4
RK4 63.6
Exact (analytic) 63.7

Summary & Insights

RK4 provides higher accuracy compared to Euler’s method due to its higher-order corrections, making it better suited for precise calculations. Euler’s method, while simpler, tends to accumulate more error over steps.

Second-Order Differential Equations

Second-order DEs involve the second derivative and model oscillations, circuits, and mechanical systems. They are essential for understanding vibrations, waves, and stability.

General Linear Form

\[ a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + b\frac{dy}{dx} + cy = f(x) \]

Homogeneous Solution

\[ y = C_1 e^{x} + C_2 e^{2x} \]

Describes oscillations in springs, circuits, and more.

Chapter 3: Partial Differentiation

In this chapter, we transitioned from single-variable calculus to multivariable analysis. We learned to interpret and compute changes in systems with more than one variable, laying the foundation for fields like thermodynamics, machine learning, and fluid mechanics.

Functions of Several Variables

Functions like f(x, y) and f(x, y, z) model multidimensional phenomena: topographic maps, economic surfaces, temperature distributions, etc. We visualized these as surfaces in 3D space and discussed their domain and range.

Limits and Continuity

We explored how multivariable limits can vary depending on the path. For example, we used polar coordinates to examine if a limit exists at the origin by approaching along different curves—straight lines, parabolas, or spirals. We also learned about continuity in higher dimensions and the subtle behaviors of surface functions at singularities.

Partial Derivatives

Partial derivatives measure how a function changes with respect to one variable while keeping others constant. We computed:
\( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \), and higher-order derivatives like \( \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} \).
They are essential in studying wave motion, optimization, and thermodynamic systems.

Gradient and Directional Derivatives

The gradient vector \( \nabla f = \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \right) \) points toward the direction of steepest increase. We learned how directional derivatives use a unit vector to compute the rate of change in any direction using:
\( D_{\vec{u}} f = \nabla f \cdot \vec{u} \)
We applied this in terrain slope modeling, machine learning loss surfaces, and magnetic fields.

Tangent Planes and Linear Approximations

We approximated functions near a point using their tangent planes, similar to linearization in single-variable calculus:
\( L(x, y) = f(a, b) + f_x(a, b)(x - a) + f_y(a, b)(y - b) \)

Optimization and Extrema

Using the first and second partial derivatives, we found local extrema by solving \( f_x = f_y = 0 \). The discriminant:
\( D = f_{xx}f_{yy} - (f_{xy})^2 \)
helped us classify points as local maxima, minima, or saddle points. We explored examples like profit maximization and minimum cost structures.

Lagrange Multipliers

We applied this method to optimize functions under constraints:
\( \nabla f = \lambda \nabla g \)
used in engineering design problems, like maximizing volume with a surface area constraint. This concept is fundamental in optimization algorithms used in AI.

Chapter 4: Partial Differential Equations

This chapter focused on solving real-world systems described by Partial Differential Equations (PDEs), where analytical solutions are not feasible. We learned numerical methods to simulate heat flow, wave propagation, and equilibrium states using computers.

Classification of PDEs

PDEs are classified based on the discriminant \( B^2 - AC \). This determines the nature of solutions:

  • Elliptic: \( B^2 - AC < 0 \) → e.g., Laplace's equation (steady-state heat, electrostatics)
  • Parabolic: \( B^2 - AC = 0 \) → e.g., Heat equation (diffusion over time)
  • Hyperbolic: \( B^2 - AC > 0 \) → e.g., Wave equation (vibrations, acoustics)

Finite Difference Approximations

We approximated derivatives using finite differences. These were key to converting PDEs into algebraic forms that can be computed iteratively:

  • Forward: \( f'(x) \approx \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \)
  • Backward: \( f'(x) \approx \frac{f(x) - f(x-h)}{h} \)
  • Central: \( f'(x) \approx \frac{f(x+h) - f(x-h)}{2h} \)
  • Second-order: \( f''(x) \approx \frac{f(x+h) - 2f(x) + f(x-h)}{h^2} \)

Heat Equation (Parabolic PDE)

We used the explicit (Bendre-Schmidt) method and implicit (Crank-Nicolson) scheme:

Explicit: \( u_i^{n+1} = \lambda u_{i-1}^n + (1 - 2\lambda) u_i^n + \lambda u_{i+1}^n \)

\( \lambda = \frac{\alpha \Delta t}{(\Delta x)^2} \), stable if \( \lambda \leq 0.5 \)

Crank-Nicolson offers better accuracy and unconditional stability but requires solving linear systems.

Wave Equation (Hyperbolic PDE)

We modeled vibrations using:

\( u_i^{n+1} = 2(1 - \lambda^2)u_i^n + \lambda^2(u_{i+1}^n + u_{i-1}^n) - u_i^{n-1} \)

Where \( \lambda = \frac{c\Delta t}{\Delta x} \). This was applied to simulate strings and sound waves.

Laplace Equation (Elliptic PDE)

To solve steady-state problems, we used iterative methods:

  • Standard 5-point: \( u_{i,j} = \frac{1}{4}(u_{i+1,j} + u_{i-1,j} + u_{i,j+1} + u_{i,j-1}) \)
  • Diagonal 5-point: For more accuracy in diagonal influence
  • Gauss-Seidel and Successive Over-Relaxation to accelerate convergence

Summary Table

Type PDE Method Stability
Parabolic Heat Equation Explicit, Crank-Nicolson Conditional, Unconditional
Hyperbolic Wave Equation Central Difference Stable if \( \lambda \leq 1 \)
Elliptic Laplace Equation Iterative, SOR Converges Iteratively

Applications:

  • Simulating thermal diffusion in heat exchangers (Parabolic)
  • Modeling earthquake waves (Hyperbolic)
  • Solving electrostatics and fluid flow problems (Elliptic)

Real-Life Applications of Differential Equations

  • Cooling and Heating: Newton’s Law of Cooling models how hot objects cool down (e.g., coffee, forensic science, industrial cooling).
  • Population Growth: Exponential and logistic equations model how populations or bacteria grow and stabilize.
  • Finance: Compound interest, loan amortization, and investment growth are modeled using first-order DEs.
  • Physics: Second-order DEs describe motion (projectiles), spring-mass-damper systems, and electrical circuits (RLC circuits).
  • Medicine: Drug concentration in the bloodstream over time (pharmacokinetics).
  • Engineering: Control systems, signal processing, and vibration analysis all rely on DEs.
  • Chemistry: Reaction rates and radioactive decay are governed by first-order DEs.

Summary: Differential equations are everywhere! From cooling coffee to predicting epidemics, they help us understand and shape the world.
